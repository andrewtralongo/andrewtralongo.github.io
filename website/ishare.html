Introduction


8th of August, 2017

Personalisation, digital manufacturing and mass production; these were the overarching themes asked to research. I was intrigued by the concept of turning the physical digital instead of the digital physical, and while beginning to brainstorm and develop initial ideas of what to research, I looked at my arms, my tattoos and was inspired: the concept of a tattoo. As the body can be considered as the ultimate mass produced object, furthermore body adornment in turn the most common form of personalisation, it seemed suitable to follow this stream of thought. Researching into digital tattoos, I came across QR code and RFID tattoos, small chips implanted beneath the skin. The first RFID tattoo contained a simple rainbow gradient gif which raises the question; is the chip considered to be the tattoo or the the gradient gif which it holds? Is the tattoo the QR code or the website it links to? If one couldn't access the internet or if it ceased to exist, would the tattoo? The ink or chip would still physically exist so what would it then represent? A mark of the existence of what once was or could have been? The concept of a digital presence, an avatar, a whole other realm of existence where the possibilities are endless. One which has quickly intertwined itself into the everyday, furthermore the consequences which have developed from it.

A whole new world


23rd of September, 2017

The notion of personalisation, transformation and adornment has always existed within our culture. In the beginning many inscribed the body with symbols and glyphs for religious purposes, manipulating them in order to complete rituals and communicate to gods and spirits. Tattoos originated as a form of identification, and for sailors acted as a journal of their travels and worldly experiences. Although in this digital era, personalisation and adornment have transformed into the form of avatars, the representation of our digital-self; this could be something as simple as your Facebook profile picture or a whole blog which you post to once a day. This is not to say that the importance of traditional decor has diminished, yet the value of an avatar has quickly grown to carry an as equal significance. As it is easier to personalise your avatar to your desire, it has become the depiction of how we would like to be idealistically perceived. Our avatars are manufactured and designed instead of a depiction of our true selves.

19rd of August, 2017

FAT LAB experimented this importance of social media with their piece Social Roulette. Only taking 4 hours to fully produce, all you do is simply sign in to the site with your Facebook account and play Russian Roulette with your account. The only rules are that you must use your own account to play and only play once a day; if you lose, your account is deleted. It was inspired by two websites, one which would delete 1 to 10 of your Facebook friends on random and another which would ask you to delete 5 friends to get a free Whopper. During the short existence of Social Roulette’s life, 393 people participated. Theoretically 65 people should have died given the 1 in 6 odds yet all of them survived. Within 4 hours of the site’s launch, Facebook removed their API key access stopping the website from working and in the end, the website couldn’t actually delete your account. It was a performance disguised as a game. A comment on our digital identities and its ephemeral nature, that it can all be deleted within the blink of an eye. Highlighting the true importance that it possesses in our lives.

Alone Together


23rd of September, 2017

With the intertwined nature of social media, this dependency has lead to a plethora of issues amongst society; technology has become so psychologically powerful that it doesn’t change what we do, but who we are. We have become so addicted to the concept of receiving likes that it directly drives what we share online and therefore what we do in the everyday. Where we go for lunch. Who we see for a few drinks. What we choose our avatar to be, look and behave can all affect how others perceive you, and even change how you perceive yourself. If someone were to wear a black uniform, they would be seen as aggressive. On the contrary if they were to wear a white uniform, a symbol of piece. If you were to choose your avatar to wear this black uniform, others would naturally expect you to be hostile and troublesome. Even if you may not be, you may begin to abide to this stereotype as others may begin to fear and be weary of you solely based on these assumptions; this is called Behavioural Confirmation, where the expectations of the perceiver cause the target to behave in ways that conform to the perceiver’s expectations. This applies to the avatar as it is an entire self-representation over all forms of adornment and personalisation, not simply a piece of clothing. Although in an online presence, it is not as easy to know of the expectations and thoughts of others as the screen and text has replaced true face-to-face communication. Therefore the target begins to assume these presumptions during self-perception and begin to conform to the behaviour that they believe others would expect them to have, this called the Proteus Effect. We have become so obsessed to the perceptions, thoughts and ideals of others that it has begun to control us, who we are and how we act.

In being always connected and in communication with others, it has in turn made us isolated as a society. The ability to control who and how often we communicate with each other has caused us to fear simple face-to-face conversations. This fundamental social skill has become daunting to so many, triggering symptoms of anxiety, depression and obsessive-compulsive disorder. You cannot edit, delete or refine what you are going to say; you become your true self. Express your true feelings, thoughts and emotions. “I’d rather text than talk” shows that we have grown fear in humanity and self-confidence. We have learnt to expect more from technology and less from others. We’re lonely yet afraid of intimacy, designing the illusion of companionship without the demands of friendship. We turn to social media to escape not only from the world around us, but from ourselves. Our thoughts, our demons and our issues. The online has become the ideal realm of existence, and rather than trying to make the real world a happier one, we often escape to the digital to produce how we want to appear rather than how we truly do.

Disconnected


14th of August, 2017

In order to control this addiction and behaviour, for one 24 hour period in March every year since 2012, people in America have participated in National Day of Unplugging. A 24 hour digital detox. In 2014, one writer from The New Yorker, Casey N. Cep, criticised the entire purpose of this day, simply calling it “pointless”. Cep discusses how the concept of the real world is insidious, suggesting that the online presence, an avatar, isn’t authentic. It is meaningless and insignificant. “Unplugging is the latest effort to escape the everyday.” With how interconnected we are, it is difficult to imagine a world without the digital, often forgetting about the benefits of technology. Cep acknowledges her appreciation of these benefits by sharing an anecdote of how she found out about her grandmother’s death while travelling, and if not for the internet, would have missed the funeral and wouldn’t of known until her return. “Unplugging from devices doesn’t stop us from experiencing our lives through their lenses, frames, and formats. We are only ever tourists in the land of no technology…” Arguing that those who unplug don’t stay in this ‘land’ for very long and have every intention of returning, Cep’s argument is that we should reflect and consider how to live technology better rather than pretend to live without them for a day.

140 Characters


14th of August, 2017

While this dependency to a screen may be perceived as controlled behaviour, it can be compulsive, a chemical addiction. Research has shown that when you receive a notification, Dopamine is released in your system. This endorphin is a chemical released by neurons to send signals to other nerve cells, and with distinct dopamine pathways in the brain, it plays a major role in reward-motivated behaviour. Many addictions increase dopamine activity, which is the cause of a ‘high’ and coincidently the comedown. Sharon Begley, the author of Can’t Just Stop: An Investigation of Compulsions, says that “The emerging idea seems to be that when reality falls short, we feel a dopamine plunge. That feels bad, so we keep trying to do something that will make reality live up to expectations.” We are constantly trying to make our lives seem better than they are through social media, by only sharing the best of the best. Through our avatar, creating an image rather than a reflection. Begley says that this rush isn’t the actual notification; it is in fact the anticipation of receiving one. The anticipation that your phone will light up and buzz, knowing that someone craves your attention as much as you crave theirs. She believes if we want to be less dependent on our devices that we need to identify the true source of our anxiety. We need to stop escaping into the digital.

Conclusion


25th of August, 2017

With the digital being used in such a variety of ways, how should we approach it from now on? The technology has evolved so rapidly and integrated itself within the everyday that we cannot deny its existence nor the responsibility which we have to control its addiction, how it is changing us as people. As a society. The ephemeral nature of the digital has such a lasting impression on the physical that both are now forever intertwined. The duality of the digital avatar versus our ‘real’ identity, manufacturing our image. We must accept reality and stop escaping.
